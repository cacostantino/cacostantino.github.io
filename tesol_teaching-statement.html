TESOL Teaching Statement

Teaching Purpose

I began learning a second language (Spanish) in middle school, but it wasn’t until my junior year of high school that I really became interested in learning languages. The summer before my junior year, I spent a month in Spain with the family of the exchange student we had hosted the previous summer. While my host family spoke English quite well, they really pushed me to speak Spanish to take advantage of being immersed in the Spanish culture and environment. This experience also encouraged me to add Latin to my high school curriculum, and later move to Milan to study Italian. Now I am a lover of languages; I have collected dictionaries of and dabbled in Irish, Sicilian, Portuguese, Dutch, Thai and recently studied French during a short stay in Montréal, QC, Canada in July 2017.

Teaching Style

My teaching style is based mostly on the Communicative Approach (1980). This approach appeals to me because it works for everyone and really focuses on effective and efficient communication (listening, speaking, reading, and writing) while keeping culture in mind. I like to use real-world materials, group/pair work, and games to really get students engaged and make learning fun.

I want to focus on teaching adults in conversational, college-prep and business English.  With conversational beginners, I will use the Comprehension Approach (1980) to help students become comfortable with listening and understanding English before pushing them to speak, by using visual dictionaries and labels, short simple sentences and observable actions. I also love to incorporate music, art and other forms of creativity from the Affective Humanistic Approach (1970) to allow students to become more fully immersed in language and culture, and engage other parts of the brain so that language is acquired more naturally; it shouldn’t always need to feel like work. These modalities can tap into students’ interests and help them connect with the language in a way that otherwise might not be achieved. With more advanced students, especially those preparing for college and the business world, I also look to the Direct Approach (1910) and use dialogues, conversations and visual aids to simulate real-world situations.

I will best meet the needs of my students by being adaptable. Each student is unique and has different needs and desires for learning English. As such, I get to know my students as quickly as possible so that I can adapt my lessons to match their needs and interests. Finally, creating a comfortable, enjoyable, and fun environment is beneficial and motivating to all.

I want to teach English to share my love of languages and culture to those who need/want to learn my native language and the American culture. As a lifelong learner of languages, I understand how and why students of English make the mistakes they do, and so I feel this gives me the empathy and appreciation needed to support ESL/EFL students. My short-term teaching goal is to provide a solid foundation of English (spoken, written, comprehension, expression) to meet students’ immediate goals, and the tools to continue their English language development to more advanced levels. My long-term goal is to encourage students to become lifelong learners and to seek new knowledge. To learn a new language and culture is to gain a new perspective on life and humanity.

Teaching Techniques

I use several teaching techniques in my classes. As I have learned through Toastmasters Intl., it is important to grab the audience’s attention from the very beginning. So, I like to start each class with a story, music, current event, or interesting discussion to get students primed for the day’s lesson.  I also like to humor and warm language to keep students engaged throughout the lesson. As I have mentioned previously, it’s important to me to get to know my students so that I can weave their backgrounds, experience, interests and desires into lessons as appropriate. Students learn faster and remember more when they can cognitively, emotionally and socially connect with learning material. Another technique I have borrowed from my experience with Toastmasters, is to set the expectation that the classroom is a safe and encouraging space so that students feel comfortable to take risks and make mistakes.

I try to employ the 80 (practice) / 20 (teaching) rule as much as possible because, as I know from my personal language acquisition experiences, it’s imperative to practice, practice, practice and then practice some more. I find guided practice and independent practice (in pairs and alone) works well and gives me the opportunity to listen and provide feedback individually. I do offer quizzes and short tests to give me a sense of what I need to review, and to provide the students a sense of how much they have really internalized.
